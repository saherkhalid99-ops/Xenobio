# XenoBio 🔴
### A Decision-Support Tool Matching Earth's Extremophile Bacteria to Mars and Space Environments

🔗 **Live Tool:** [Open XenoBio](https://saherkhalid99-ops.github.io/Xenobio/xenobio-v1.html)

---

## What is XenoBio?

XenoBio is a free, open-access decision-support tool for astrobiologists, planetary scientists, and mission planners. It matches Earth's extremophile bacteria — both natural and engineered strains — to Mars and space environments based on published survival and functional traits from peer-reviewed astrobiology literature.

---

## Key Features

- 🦠 **30 extremophile strains** — natural isolates and engineered constructs with documented space-relevant traits
- 🪐 **8 space environments** — Mars Equatorial Day/Night, Mars Polar, Mars Subsurface, Pressurized Habitat, Lab Simulant, Lunar Surface, ISS — with confirmed NASA mission parameters
- 📚 **50 verified references** from *Astrobiology*, *Nature Biotechnology*, *Science*, *Applied and Environmental Microbiology*, *Nature Reviews Microbiology*, and NASA technical reports
- 🚀 **Explorer Mode** — plain language matching for mission planners and students
- 🔬 **Researcher Mode** — full technical strain data, Earth analog environments, evidence quality ratings, and literature citations per strain
- ⚗️ **Purpose-based filtering** — Perchlorate Bioremediation, Space Agriculture (PGPR), Nitrogen Fixation, Radiation Resistance, Desiccation Tolerance, ISRU/Life Support
- 💻 **Works offline** — no login, no installation, no cost

---

## How to Use

1. Open the [live tool](https://saherkhalid99-ops.github.io/Xenobio/xenobio-v1.html)
2. Select a space environment (e.g. Mars Equatorial Day, Mars Subsurface, ISS)
3. Select your research purpose (e.g. Perchlorate Bioremediation, Space Agriculture)
4. Toggle between Explorer Mode or Researcher Mode
5. Get ranked extremophile candidates with match scores and literature support instantly

---

## Scientific Basis

All strain–environment matches reflect published experimental evidence from peer-reviewed astrobiology, microbiology, and planetary science literature. Mars environment parameters are derived from Phoenix Lander, Curiosity Rover, and Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter mission data (Hecht et al. 2009; Horneck et al. 2010). This tool is a knowledge synthesis resource — not a guarantee of survival or functional outcomes in actual space conditions.
